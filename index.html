Jobs lost in MA in the pandemic

By Marina Villeneuve

How have Massachusetts' public and private sectors fared since the pandemic?

I looked at Bureau of Labor Statistics data to get some insight.

BLS data isn't perfect, and there are lots of different ways to gauge how well the economy is doing now compared to pre-pandemic.

But sector-specific employment data can provide crucial context about which parts of the economy are seeing growth or declines. And it could be useful to simply compare where MA jobs stand according to the latest data, so I'm looking at May 2023 to May 2019. Picking this time frame as a comparison also could help address the ups and down of seasonal governmental employment.

Looking at the raw employmenet data can also shed light on the reality of the job market at a time when various industries who say they've been hit by the pandemic are pushing for new laws, regulations or assistance. The pandemic indeed hit a blow to many industries -- but which ones are seeing a healthy uptick in employment? This could reveal which industries perhaps are not in as dire straits as lobbyists may otherwise argue.

A 2021 BLS report found that state and local government also had high job losses -- with a 5.1% drop for state government and 6.5% for local government from March 2020 to March 2021. That's compared with 4.3% for the private sector.

Wages are an important part of the puzzle, the report highlights: "Compared with the private sector, state and local government had lower mean wages for high-paying occupational groups such as management, legal, and computer and mathematical occupations." And state government shows a pattern of paying workers in higher paying occupations significantly less compared to the private sector, according to BLS.

I found that both the private and local government sectors have rebounded in MA. Both sectors saw a 1% increase in jobs from May 2019 to May 2023 -- with an increase of 43,400 private sector jobs and 1,300 local government jobs. The local government sector largely employs teachers, librarians, administrators and protective services employees like police.

However, the state government sector in MA has yet to regain all jobs lost in the pandemic.

Nationally, state government makes up just 3% of overall employment nationally, according to BLS. And the largest occupation in state government across the US: correctional officers.

Massachusetts state government is only at 93% of pre-pandemic employment levels.

State government has seen a 7 % drop: meaning a loss of 9,100 state government jobs in Massachusetts.

The MA state government education sector saw an 11% drop of 7,300 jobs -- this sector includes postsecondary teaching assistants and postsecondary health specialties teachers.

BLS says that postsecondary teachers and teaching assistants had lower wages in state government nationally than in the private sector: "law, criminal justice, and social work postsecondary teachers earned an average of $24,800 less in state government than in the private sector in May 2020." Part of the reason for the disparity: state government had a lower share of law teachers.

Other hard-hit sectors include state government hospitals, which lost 800 jobs for a 11% drop.

The drop in jobs at state government hospitals got me curious about how the hospital sector is faring overall.

The hospital sector overall rebounded in the pandemic...

but I found that nursing homes have yet to recover.
